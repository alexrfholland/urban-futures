# Abstract \[239\]

Urban environments support diverse biological communities, yet dominant design practices exclude nonhuman contributions and reinforce human-led futures that produce cumulative harm. This article responds by reframing leadership as probabilistic pathway redirection rather than intentional human control. We define design as the collective capacity to engage with novelty and propose nonhuman leadership as a way to influence future outcomes. In this approach, nonhuman beings generate proposals that expand the space of possible futures. We operationalise this reframing in a spatiotemporal model that expresses nonhuman influence through empirically informed personas and makes their proposals legible for long-term assessment. The model does not assign leadership. Instead, it functions as an arena where humans can see the consequences of supporting or resisting nonhuman proposals. We test the approach in Melbourne, Australia, where birds, lizards and trees propose alternative trajectories for the urban forest from 2025 to 2205. More-than-human communities may enable or resist these proposals, shaping pathways to alternative futures. We compare a human-led pathway that follows current practices with a nonhuman-led pathway that incorporates nonhuman proposals. Results show that human-led pathways fail to halt degradation. In contrast, nonhuman-led pathways redirect trajectories toward greater biomass, biodiversity and multispecies benefits through proposals such as canopy retention, preserved decay, structural reuse and the release of human control over ecosystem processes. The study offers a way to extend futures practice beyond human-authored scenarios and reveals transformative possibilities that remain inaccessible through trend-based modelling of design outcomes.

# Introduction \[679\]

Futures scholarship increasingly argues that responses to environmental crisis must generate alternative futures that diverge from existing trajectories (Cork et al. 2023). International assessments describe this need as "transformation," a shift in scope, speed and depth of change (O'Brien et al. 2025). Participatory scenarios, visions and pathways can contribute to transformative sustainability planning, biodiversity policy and urban governance by surfacing plural values, exploring uncertainty and supporting deliberation under changing conditions (Neuhoff et al. 2023). More-than-human futures scholarship extends this work by calling for the inclusion of nonhuman collectives (Bina et al. 2024; Sheikh et al. 2023; Pedroso-Roussado et al. 2025), supported by design approaches that show how humans, animals, plants and ecosystems build futures together (Roudavski 2021; Heitlinger et al. 2025). However, even approaches aligned with plural human--nature relations often retain anthropocentric assumptions that restrict alternative possibilities (Mansur et al. 2022).

Recent critiques highlight this limitation. Scenario-based methods can privilege human intentionality, consensus and institutional governance, which narrows the possibility space even when participants seek transformative change (Stirling 2019; Sharpe et al. 2016). The Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services Transformative Change Assessment finds that many futures practices normalise degradation by presenting transformation as unrealistic, risky or politically unattainable (O'Brien et al. 2025). As a result, many exercises in futures modelling stabilise incremental trajectories instead of redirecting them.

Urban environments make these tensions visible. Cities support diverse biological communities, yet dominant planning and management practices prioritise human safety, efficiency and aesthetic control (Prebble et al. 2021; Gabrys et al. 2022). Even wildlife-inclusive, nature-based and biodiversity-sensitive design frameworks often treat nonhuman beings as stakeholders, beneficiaries or constraints rather than contributors to futures (Garrard et al. 2018; Apfelbeck et al. 2020; Hernandez-Santin et al. 2022). Futures work that builds on these frameworks typically consults nonhuman life indirectly by using indicators and values to inform human actions instead of treating indicators as ways to interpret nonhuman processes that can shape futures themselves (Roudavski and Brock 2025).

This article reframes nonhuman beings as leaders in futures processes. We define leadership not in terms of agents' power to make individual decisions but as a collective process that alters the probability of which options become chosen. This definition draws on futures approaches that emphasise emergence and redirection of pathways rather than prediction or optimisation (Sharpe et al. 2016). In our framing, leadership bends trajectories over time instead of selecting endpoints. It can arise from nonhuman beings through their interactions with environments, infrastructures and other species (Rutten et al. 2024).

Design provides a concrete futuring practice through which nonhuman beings can lead. Our previous work shows that collaborations with owls, trees and other nonhuman beings can generate beneficial designs that human-led approaches overlook (Holland and Roudavski 2024; Parker et al. 2022). These studies led us to define design as the collective creation of conditions that support life in response to novelty (Roudavski 2021; Rutten et al. 2024). These interactions may be described as more-than-human, interspecies or multispecies. Here, we refer to them as nonhuman-led design.

We propose nonhuman leadership as a futures method. Instead of presenting scenarios or visions authored by humans, this method enables nonhuman beings to generate proposals that apply directional pressure to pathways. Throughout this article, we use the term community to describe shifting constellations of beings, processes and infrastructures that design futures together (Holland and Roudavski 2024). Subsets of these communities make proposals through growth, decay and trial behaviours. They then evaluate such proposals through uptake, resistance or conflict. This framing challenges futures practices that locate agency solely in human deliberation or governance by extending debates on plural, contested and emergent futures. (Cork et al. 2023; Neuhoff et al. 2023).

To test this approach, we develop a spatiotemporal model of leadership for Melbourne, Australia, where birds, lizards and trees propose alternative trajectories for the urban forest over long timeframes. By comparing a human-led pathway that aligns with current management practice with a nonhuman-led pathway that enables nonhuman proposals, the study shows how nonhuman leadership can expand the space of possible futures and reveal transformative outcomes that trend-based approaches cannot capture.

# Approach \[1097\]

This section outlines the conceptual foundations of nonhuman leadership as a futures method. It situates our modelling approach within debates on pathways, visions, participation, and justice. We treat the model as an analytical arena, not a decision-making system. The arena makes leadership effects visible over time. The approach extends pathway-based futures methods by representing nonhuman propositional agency in a structured, testable form.

## Thriving, justice and futures evaluation

The study evaluates alternative futures using the concept of thriving as a relational condition shared across human and nonhuman communities. Thriving refers to the capacity of organisms and collectives to maintain healthy states while adapting to change and sustaining resilience (Roudavski and Brock 2025; Celermajer et al. 2025). This concept underpins the IPBES 2050 Vision for Biodiversity and informs recent futures work concerned with plural values and justice (Kim et al. 2023; O'Brien et al. 2025). Thriving depends on environments that enable organisms and collectives to express their capabilities including freedoms and opportunities to live good lives (Nussbaum 2011; Parker et al. 2022).

Thriving is unevenly distributed across futures pathways. Historical contingencies, infrastructures, policy regimes, and cultural norms shape which beings can influence trajectories and which face systemic constraints (Bina et al. 2024). Futures practices always involve participation, but they do not distribute it evenly (Neuhoff et al. 2023). Building on procedural approaches to multispecies justice, we treat thriving as a process that expands opportunities to participate in shaping shared futures, rather than as a determined destination (Roudavski and Brock 2025). This approach enables the assessment of futures based not only on final outcomes, but also on inclusion or omission of contributions by affected lifeforms.

## Leadership as probabilistic redirection

Within this framework, leadership increases the probability that a community can thrive. Distributed approaches locate leadership not in individuals or institutions, but in interactions within collectives (Coenen et al. 2020; Garfield et al. 2019). Successful leadership expands what we have previously defined as design potential (Roudavski and Holland 2026). We conceptualise this here as a "possibility space," the range of options a community can sense, generate, and coordinate when responding to novelty. Within this space, some options support thriving, a smaller region contains known options, and a still smaller region lies within practical reach. In this view, attributes such as consciousness, intelligence, and the ability to lead describe properties distributed across a community (Giaccardi et al. 2026), not traits of individuals.

In this framing, nonhuman leadership is collective, propositional, and directional. It is collective because organisms, cells, genes, and other organic agents reconfigure the possibility space in ways that expand futures in some conditions and contract them in others (Holland and Roudavski 2024). Rather than relying on human control or executing discrete interventions, it works by shifting relationships within collectives. To represent these relationships, we distinguish two interrelated communities:

-   The propositional community includes beings and processes that express capabilities with directional valency. Human proposals emerge through text, drawing, and action. Nonhuman proposals arise through growth, decline, and trial behaviours. Actions of this community extend the scope of exploration.

-   The evaluative community includes beings and processes that respond to proposals. Humans respond in ways that include direct experience to numerical assessment, storytelling, and negotiation. Nonhuman entities respond through actions that include uptake, avoidance, repurposing, and decay. Actions of this community direct exploration toward viable destinations.

This framework accepts that both human and nonhuman beings can propose futures within a shared process. It defines leadership is the probabilistic redirection that emerges from the between propositions and evaluations.

## Pathways as futures comparison

To operationalise nonhuman leadership as a futures method, we introduce a spatiotemporal model that simulates pathways through a portion of the possibility space, including through regions human planners neither define nor consider (Figure 1). Pathways describe sequences of states connected by interventions. This approach aligns with futures methods that emphasise emergence, lock-in effects and redirection over time rather than static end states or normative visions (Saritas 2013; Sharpe et al. 2016; Pacini and Bauknecht 2025).

To adapt such methods to show differences between human-led and nonhuman-led futures, we draw on more-than-human scholarship that understands forests as political entities created by interactions between trees, arboreal dwellers, fungus, humans, institutions, future visions, algorithms and data (Westerlaken et al. 2022; Gabrys et al. 2022). We extend this work by treating modelling as a visibility device that reveals interactions in a community over time as paths (Figure 1, grey paths). The model does not decide or allocate leadership but functions as an arena in which proposals and responses accumulate into pathway redirection (Figure 1, orange paths). To trace these effects, the model generates "states" and "destinations" as observable slices of pathways at a point in time (Figure 1, light purple dashes). This approach makes nonhuman leadership legible and comparable over long horizons without prescribing preferred or human-authored end points.

The model uses empirically grounded personas to help interpret the propositional community. These personas represent taxa as collectives rather than individuals, drawing from ecological data on habitat features, lifecycles and relationships. Unlike personas used in participatory design or scenario workshops, they do not simulate preferences, intentions or narratives (Apfelbeck et al. 2020). Instead, their purpose is to focus pluralistic perspectives into coherent proxies for group-level tendencies. They do so by converting distributed capabilities, stakes and tendencies into measurable relationship that can then be used to define proposals for change (Figure 1, light purple).

To make the evaluative community legible within the model, we focus on one dimension, the difficulty of change. Socio-ecological modellers often describe this as resistance (Mansur et al. 2022). We extend such work by conceptualising resistance as a force arising from biophysical conditions, infrastructure, policy and norms that tilts probabilities toward trend-aligned pathways (Figure 1, blue triangles). Other regions of the possibility space contain their own tendencies and properties, but the push toward the trending path means these regions often remain undiscovered by current practices (Figure 1, pink triangles).

This approach allows us to evaluate futures through pathway comparison rather than scenario selection. In this evaluation, leadership does not execute outcomes but generates directional pressure that counters resistance (Figure 1, white arrows). When proposals gain support, whether through ecological uptake, institutional acceptance or material compatibility, communities translate them into sequences of interventions that protect, restore, enhance or manage biodiversity (Figure 1, dark purple). The model exposes adjustable controls so that human users can observe different pathways that emerge from patterns of support and rejection.

# Methods \[2225\]

We use modelling to support a futures argument rather than to offer a comprehensive habitat suitability assessment. To do so, we compare a human-led pathway consistent with current practice with a nonhuman-led pathway that increases the likelihood that nonhuman proposals influence pathway formation. The comparison measures improvement possible through each pathway, then interprets the divergence as evidence that nonhuman propositional direction expands the possibility space beyond human-led scenario practice.

The method links two components: a possibility space that defines potential states over time, and a pathway simulation that explores sequences of change within it. The study reports results at five evaluation years: 0, 10, 30, 60, and 180. We use year 180 as a shared endpoint consistent with succession horizons for mature eucalyptus canopies (Le Roux et al. 2014).

To locate these features within the urban form, we define a "habitat volume" as the subset of the possibility space that the model represents at each site across space and time. Pathway states represent the condition of this volume at a time step. The model builds the initial state from geographic information systems, lidar, and canopy footprint data, classifying voxels as urban elements and grouping these into facades, roof segments, canopy footprints, and other structures. The model computes two spatial metrics it uses to determine which interventions are feasible. "Feature resistance" aggregates values beneath habitat features using aerial canopy footprint data. "Connectivity resistance" applies a cellular automata algorithm to ground, facade, and roof surfaces based on methods from landscape ecology (Hesselbarth et al. 2024). These metrics allow the model to estimate effort required to support nonhuman proposals, whether through modifying individual features or improving connections between them.

### Baseline conditions

We establish a baseline state by simulating ecological conditions prior to intensive human disturbance in southeastern Australia circa 1700. The model disables resistance values and distributes habitat features with origin set to precolonial and management set to unmanaged across the volume. These distributions match benchmark estimates for each lifecycle phase (Gibbons et al. 2010). This reference volume supports comparison with current and future conditions.

## Novel pathways \[821\]

With the possibility space defined, we simulate how the propositional and evaluative communities drive pathways through it.[^3] The model generates proposals from personas, translates community responses into interventions, differentiates pathways through varying levels of support, and produces outputs for comparison.

### Design proposals

The simulation includes many processes that alter habitat volumes over time, including lifecycle progression and stochastic events such as tree death, collapse, and plant dispersal. We treat a subset of these processes as proposals put forward by personas. Each proposal corresponds to a real-world action through which a persona seeks to expand opportunities to express its capabilities (Figure 5).

We define five proposals that represent major choices for urban multispecies communities: "Deploy Structure", "Decay", "Recruit", "Colonise", and "Release Control". When the model triggers a proposal, it marks affected voxels and links them to the relevant proposal type.[^4] For example, the Tree persona can propose Decay for a large old feature when the model estimates that the feature will progress to the large old lifecycle phase. If the community rejects this proposal, current management continues and managers replace the feature with a sapling. Other triggers include voxels located within grassland patches (Colonise), in canopies (Release Control), on existing infrastructure such as utility poles (Deploy), and at patch edges where the cellular automata model identifies candidate options for expansion (Colonise). These proposals allow the model to represent opportunities for personas to redirect pathways and to expose the cumulative consequences of support or resistance.

### Interventions as community response

"Interventions" represent how the evaluative community responds to proposals. An intervention changes attributes of one voxel or a group of voxels in response to a proposal. The model applies interventions where voxel affordances match intervention requirements (Figure 6).

Some interventions fully support proposals. For example, "Buffer" assigns a habitat island attribute under a large old feature in response to the Decay proposal, then allows eventual collapse that benefits all personas. Other interventions provide partial support. "Brace" supports Decay by preventing removal of large old and standing dead features but does not provide space for collapse, which limits benefits for ground dwellers.

Whether an intervention proceeds depends on its "effort threshold," a value between 0 and 100 set by human users to represent community support. The model compares resistance at voxels marked by a proposal with the threshold and allocates an intervention when the threshold exceeds resistance. Interventions use either feature or connectivity resistance depending on whether they target individual habitat features or connections. When multiple interventions fit, the model selects the highest effort option.

This mechanism does not define leadership but instead defines receptivity and constraint, where communities enable, resist, or modify proposals. Increased thresholds do not equal leadership given that the capacity to make proposals relies on previous decisions. Effort thresholds only increase the likelihood that communities support nonhuman proposals.

### Leadership impact on pathways

Leadership, as we define it, increases the likelihood that nonhuman proposals influence pathway formation. The evaluative community's response through effort thresholds allows us to differentiate this effect across pathways. The human-led pathway increases effort thresholds slowly, aligning with current practice. The nonhuman-led pathway increases thresholds at a greater rate, enabling more proposals to translate into interventions.

Intervention availability also varies by place. Street enables Adapt and City enables Connect, focusing on distinct processes and their impacts. This choice supports comparability across place types while allowing each to demonstrate a distinct mechanism of redirection.

At each evaluation year, the model reads attributes from the previous state, determines proposals from personas, then assesses community response through resistance and threshold comparison. This cycle allows leadership effects to accumulate through time as path dependent consequences.

### Pathway comparisons and outputs

For each state, the model generates three classes of output that support futures comparison: a three-dimensional representation showing the distribution of habitat features within the volume; indicator totals for each persona expressed as totals and percentages relative to baseline values; and secondary metrics that identify opportunities for personas to make proposals, track how proposals convert into interventions, and quantify the effort this requires, such as urban area reallocated to support proposals. These outputs support direct comparison between pathways and allow assessment of whether nonhuman led direction yields transformative change beyond trend-aligned management.

Its evaluative community comprises biotic and abiotic ecosystem processes. # Discussion \[2977 with table, \~2650 without table\]

Extending scenario planning to include nonhuman propositional agency rather than human-authored alternatives

Providing a quantitative method for comparing transformative versus incremental pathways

Reframing leadership as probabilistic redirection rather than intentional human governance

This study proposes that nonhuman beings can lead urban transformation and introduces a modelling approach to test this hypothesis. The results show that human-led pathways risk locking urban ecosystems into degraded states, whereas nonhuman-led pathways can redirect trajectories towards thriving multispecies communities. We discuss these findings in three connected steps. First, we show how futuring practice can shift from human-authored alternatives towards nonhuman propositional agency. Second, we show that the divergence between human and nonhuman-led pathways constitutes transformation. Third, we consider what this implies for policy, practice, and governance.

## Computationally-assisted nonhuman leadership \[1382 with table, 1000 without table\]

Futures scholarship already recognises that better future-making requires broader exploration of options, clearer comparison among them, and revision as conditions change (Cordova-Pozo and Rouwette 2023). Efforts including living scenarios, participatory futuring, and Nature Futures show that futuring better meets such criteria when communities can prototype future options together (Neuhoff et al. 2023; Cork et al. 2023; Kim et al. 2023). Yet these advances still concentrate leadership in human societies, institutions, and capacities. This section shows the benefits of distributing leadership across animals, plants, ecosystems, collectives, technologies, and places (Table 2).

Even the most inclusive more-than-human futures approaches also keep humans in charge of proposing and judging, whether through speculative stand-ins or by reflecting on the implications of ecosystem data (Sheikh et al. 2023; Westerlaken et al. 2022; Gabrys et al. 2022). Distributing leadership across propositional and evaluative communities changes this arrangement (Table 2, entities row). As contexts change, roles can shift, so leadership appears less as human reflection or command than as patterned influence expressed through care, reciprocity, symbiosis, and adaptive capacity (Roudavski and Holland 2026). Leadership then becomes the process by which proposals gain or lose force within a community, rather than a property of intentional human governance.

This approach enlarges the space of futures under consideration by expanding what can count as a proposal and by distributing evaluation more evenly. Nonhuman capabilities, trials, and latent potentials extend proposals over long horizons (Table 2, proposal row), rather than the 25-year average reported across 103 urban ecosystem service scenario studies (Liu and Wu 2022). The evaluative community then determines which proposals translate into interventions through uptake, avoidance, repurposing, obstruction, nesting, decay trajectories, fungal colonisation, and other relational traces (Table 2, evaluation row). This mechanism for finding viable and desirable destinations contrasts with approaches that widen consideration while still treating nature as a managed resource (Mansur et al. 2022), or that assess nonhuman beings only through instrumental values, as in 96% of the quantitative scenarios reviewed by IPBES (O'Brien et al. 2025). In such approaches, nonhuman proposals may register as signals or values, but institutional filtering still redirects their pressure into outcomes that reassert anthropocentric priorities, especially under uncertainty or political resistance.

Communities can also delegate evaluative functions such as sensing, comparison, translation, accountability. Models represent one such form of delegation. Our model does not capture the full scope of leadership processes, but it differs from approaches that seek clearer comparison through measurement while treating urban forests as governable objects in the form of carbon stores, biodiversity reserves, or ecosystem services (Gabrys et al. 2022). Those approaches create a structural bias wherever legal, economic, or safety frameworks treat nonhuman harm as secondary, admitting nonhuman proposals only where human institutions can accommodate them without substantial redirection. At Parade, for example, familiar interventions such as shrubs, rocks, or logs could redirect pedestrian movement while reducing risk, yet managers rely on assessments that prioritise human aesthetics when deciding to remove the site's old elms (City of Melbourne 2012). The elms' proposal is thereby foreclosed before it can shape the pathway. By contrast, grounding proposals in capabilities that reflect the best possible life as the beings involved define it allows elms to explore futures towards thriving through actions such as growing, acting as substrates, and generating fallen debris. This makes visible how community interactions generate proposals, support or reject them, and shift capability indicators over time. Human experts, residents, or nonhuman beings can then interpret, contest, and act on such pathways without collapsing difference or shifting authorship back to humans (Table 2, possibility space row).

These gains in visibility do not remove risks (Table 2, risk row). Proxy and translation mechanisms can amplify nonhuman concerns but also overwrite them, a risk more-than-human approaches characterise as ventriloquism (Sheikh et al. 2023). For example, the model treats retained perch branches as suitable for Bird persona roosting. Measures such as cubic metres can translate nonhuman concerns into design guidance, but volume alone misses the connectivity, vertical layering, and physical arrangement that also shape nonhuman lives (LaRue et al. 2023). A branch counted as suitable in one location may therefore not be equivalent to a branch retained under different conditions, such as above a larger habitat island. If workflows treat these as like-for-like units, pathways can overstate success while masking differences in noise, predator pressure, and species-specific behaviours that matter to nonhuman proposers. Capabilities that depend on rare events, long temporal arcs, or diffuse relationships face even greater complications, leading pathways to favour proposals that perform well against indicators humans can model easily while undermining slower or less visible forms of thriving. To reduce these risks, protocols should require uncertainty disclosure, counter-interpretation opportunities, and periodic review of interpretation quality.

Even when protocols reduce ventriloquism, institutions can still favour proposals that remain aligned with existing regimes. The Brace intervention illustrates this pattern. It stabilises canopy presence but suppresses indicators for the Lizard persona to Communicate and Reproduce. Under the nonhuman-led pathway, institutions acknowledge the Tree persona's Decay proposal but use Brace to constrain collapse, ground disturbance, and nutrient cycling to fit risk tolerances. This creates a structural bias within the evaluative community towards partial uptake that limits exploration of the possibility space. Even when nonhuman proposals redirect pathways, downstream implementation can therefore reassert trends that the framework sought to overcome.

These limits do not undermine more-than-human futures practice. Instead, they show that its value lies less in guaranteeing just outcomes than in revealing where, how, and why communities resist nonhuman leadership, thereby exposing mechanisms for improvement (Table 2, implementation mechanisms). These include field experiments time (Holland and Roudavski 2024), legal representation and property rights over habitats such as green building envelopes (Celermajer et al. 2025), AI-enabled communication (Earth Species Project and Collective Intelligence Project 2025), or zoöp assemblies (Pedroso-Roussado et al. 2025). Such mechanisms do not resolve the risks, but they do create grounds for contestation, redesign, and learning.

## Transformative change \[895\]

We now demonstrate that the scale of divergence between human-led and nonhuman-led futures constitutes transformation. To do so, we assess three measures drawn from our capability indicators, selecting aspects where published plans can define a comparable "best practice" that improves large, old tree protection (Croeser et al. 2025), hollow availability (Le Roux et al. 2014), and site connectivity (The Nature Conservancy and Resilient Melbourne 2019). This allows us to compare the baseline reflecting states before colonisation, as well as the human-led, best practice and nonhuman-led pathways. We evaluate these measures at 10, 30 and 180 years, focusing on "old-tree persistence" at Parade, "hollow availability" at Street and "eco-connectivity" at City.

**Old-tree persistence** refers to the number of large old trees removed annually at Parade. Under the baseline conditions, removal rates remain at zero. The human-led pathway removes 3.6 trees per year until year 10, 4.8 trees per year between years 10 and 30 and 1.35 trees per year from year 30 to 180.

**Hollow availability** tracks the number of hollow-bearing structures at Street and their rate of change. The baseline conditions provide 252 structures with negligible annual variation. The human-led pathway begins with one hollow-bearing tree and adds only 0.05 to 0.12 structures per year.

**Eco-connectivity** measures the proportion of site area at City covered by grass, shrubs, open soil or understorey beneath tree canopies. The baseline conditions maintain 100% coverage. The human-led pathway reduces this to 15% at year 10 and 20% at year 30.

The best practice improves all three measures. It reduces tree removal rates by factors of 4.6, 10, and 6.5 at years 10, 30, and 180. It accelerates hollow availability 7.9 to 15.8 times faster than the human-led pathway. Eco-connectivity improves 2.1 times at year 10 and 7.6 times at year 30. These gains reflect ambitious human-led mitigation efforts. For example, Le Roux et al. (2014) anticipate the loss of many large old trees and aim to maintain hollow availability rather than exceed it.

The nonhuman-led pathway outperforms the best practice across all measures. At Parade, no trees are removed by year 10. Removal rates fall eightfold by year 30 and 500-fold by year 180 compared to the best practice. Hollow availability increases eight times faster at year 10 and 2.4 times faster at year 30. By year 180, the model adds 1.18 hollow-bearing structures annually, while the best practice loses 0.33 structures per year. This represents a rise from 1% of baseline at year 10 to 105% at year 180. The best practice begins at 33% and ends at 31%. Eco-connectivity reaches 64% and 117% of baseline at years 10 and 30, compared to best practice targets of 30--40%.

These results show that nonhuman leadership produces outcomes that diverge from human-led design, even when that design accounts for the needs of multiple species. The improvements qualify as transformative because they demonstrate the scope, speed, and depth of change required to support thriving communities rather than merely mitigate harm.

Transformation depends on changed perspectives (O'Brien et al. 2025). The nonhuman-led pathway offers such a shift through an evaluative community that supports nonhuman proposals rather than suppressing them. All communities face the task of allocating limited resources among competing proposals, yet existing cost-benefit analyses warp this allocation by comparing biodiversity gains with financial cost, human safety, certainty of outcomes, and competing land uses (Ikin et al. 2015). This bias pushes pathways toward incremental adjustment because proposals such as restoring hollow-bearing structures to baseline levels appear exceptional and risky (Le Roux et al. 2014).

This expanded view shows that communities directed by processes other than intentional human governance can preserve exploratory breadth at the level of proposals, while still creating selection, learning, and commitment mechanisms. For example, communities can delegate responsibilities to human researchers and institutions that link practice with advanced technologies to better specify shapes and materials suited to potentially viable interventions (Holland and Roudavski 2024; Weisser et al. 2023). To illustrate, the three-fold increase in hollow-bearing structures at Street arises from artificial canopies on utility poles, artificial hollows, and natural cavities in braced branches. Voxel-based analysis clarifies the scope of transformative practice by treating design as a continuous service that provides biological, hybrid, or artificial structures over time (Holland et al. 2023). Previous work offers one example of artificial hollows made from 3D-printed shells, plywood housings, and mycelium blocks with defined maintenance, reuse, and decomposition protocols (Parker, Roudavski, et al. 2025). Pilot projects can strengthen frameworks (Pedroso-Roussado et al. 2025), which can clarify combinations of proposals to scale.

Comparable pathways can also transform organisational structures by providing evidence of nonhuman contributions. Research on leadership in human societies shows that influence within a community emerges from demonstrated contributions to collective wellbeing (Garfield et al. 2020). This principle points to workflows that document gains under nonhuman leadership to strengthen the influence nonhuman beings have in overcoming resistance. Reworked public design competitions, tenders and consultations could provide communities with the means to compare conventional proposals with nonhuman-led alternatives by examining habitat structures provided, energy use, maintenance requirements and long-term contributions to multispecies capabilities.

These comparisons show that nonhuman leadership generates original pathways that achieve transformative change. Quantitative descriptions of these pathways offer measurable criteria for urban design, identify research tasks, and define spatial boundaries for functional briefs. We briefly discuss such implications for more-than-human policy, practice and governance below.

## Prospects and Policy \[596\]

Nonhuman-led design offers a transformative extension to frameworks that already focus on thriving, such as One Health and Planetary Health, but still prioritise the protection of humans from zoonotic threats. This study extends understandings that see health as a relational condition shared across human and nonhuman communities. Our model demonstrates how nonhuman proposals, such as birds guiding canopy design or trees asserting ageing rights, can benefit human as well as nonhuman wellbeing, improving air quality, temperature regulation and microbial diversity. These contributions suggest that health strategies should incorporate nonhuman agency not only as a risk factor but as a source of innovation. It is thus compatible and can add the contributions of nonhuman leadership to frameworks that already consider health as integrated but still prioritise human stewardship (SAPEA 2024).

Conservation efforts similarly stand to gain from nonhuman-led design. Traditional approaches often treat nonhuman beings as recipients of protection. In contrast, our model lets nonhuman actors propose and shape interventions, thereby expanding the scope of conservation to include novel solutions and goals. This reframing aligns conservation more closely with justice-based frameworks and strengthens its practical connection to One Health by recognising shared interests in thriving ecosystems. Nonhuman-led design can incorporate nonhuman capabilities into planning frameworks (Bush et al. 2021), integrate trials and monitoring of nonhuman proposals with monitoring schemes advised by urban forest strategies (City of Melbourne 2012; The Nature Conservancy and Resilient Melbourne 2019), and democratise such approaches to reach for more-than-human "citizen design" (Parker, Soanes, et al. 2025).

Both health and conservation fields can benefit from the technical methods introduced in this study. Research on algorithmic management shows that many organisations are building the infrastructure for artificial intelligence to assume leadership functions (Quaquebeke and Gerpott 2023). This creates openings for nonhuman agencies to enter AI-based leadership platforms as both a practical and ethical advance (Roudavski and Brock 2025). Our voxel-based modelling, persona indicators and resistance mapping offer scalable tools for evaluating multispecies futures. These methods can add to smart city and smart landscape initiatives, enabling real-time feedback, iterative design and predictive modelling that emphasise nonhuman capabilities and innovations. Recent projects suggest opportunities for interspecies communication (Prebble et al. 2021). Nonhuman-led approaches can take this work further by helping human communities reset their expectations of hygiene, aesthetics, shared use in more-than-human terms (Parker et al. 2022; Roudavski and Davis 2020).

These technical and conceptual advances also offer new opportunities for practical collaboration with Indigenous custodians. Indigenous epistemologies often conceptualise land and life as sentient and relational, aligning closely with the principles of nonhuman-led design. Our approach can support these frameworks by providing tools to operationalise multispecies governance. For instance, voxel simulations of arboreal lifecycles can support long-term stewardship practices rooted in Indigenous knowledge. Our previous work led by a culturally significant tree combined traditional knowledge with technological approaches to address nonhuman leadership and provides an example (Briggs et al. 2025).

Finally, nonhuman leadership requires changes in politics and governance that build on the extended understanding of thriving, informed by ecosystem evidence, amplified by technologies and embedded into networks of local knowledge as outlined above. Legal, educational and institutional mechanisms must evolve to protect nonhuman knowledge and support innovations proposed by nonhuman beings. This includes appointing representatives for nonhuman collectives, embedding their capabilities into planning codes, and developing funding structures that reward multispecies thriving. Existing initiatives, such as liaison officers withing 100 Resilient Cities network (Coenen et al. 2020) can serve as cases for such extensions. By formalising nonhuman leadership, cities and environments can become collaborative laboratories where all lifeforms can shape and negotiate shared futures together.

# Conclusions \[270\]

This study responds to calls from IPBES, UN agencies and futures scholarship to pursue forms of urban transformation that benefit all life rather than mitigate harm to selected species. Current urban management and futures practices continue to treat nonhuman beings as resources to control or as passive recipients of human care. This framing constrains imagination, narrows interventions to incremental adjustment, and renders transformative change improbable.

We propose an alternative by reframing nonhuman beings as leaders in futures processes. We define leadership not as authority or intention, but as a collective process that redirects the probability of choices toward desirable futures. In this framing, nonhuman leadership operates through proposals that generate directional pressure within a shared possibility space, while human roles involve enabling, resisting, or amplifying these proposals rather than authoring futures unilaterally. Transformation, in this context, involves moving from historical baselines through current degradation and then bending trajectories toward multispecies thriving.

To test this proposition, we developed a spatiotemporal model that operationalises nonhuman capabilities through empirically grounded personas and expresses leadership effects as changes in pathway probabilities over long horizons. The model does not decide outcomes or allocate leadership. Instead, it functions as an arena in which the cumulative consequences of supporting or resisting nonhuman proposals become visible and comparable. Applied to Melbourne's urban forests, the model allowed birds, lizards and trees to propose alternative trajectories and enabled systematic comparison between human-led and nonhuman-led pathways from 2025 to 2205.

The results show that human-led pathways fail to arrest degradation, while nonhuman-led pathways redirect trajectories toward increased biomass, biodiversity and multispecies benefits. Within decades, some indicators approach baseline woodland conditions, and over longer horizons, nonhuman-led pathways produce outcomes beyond the reach of human-only approaches. These findings demonstrate that nonhuman leadership can generate transformative futures that remain inaccessible under trend-based or mitigation-oriented practices.

By modelling leadership as probabilistic redirection rather than control, this study advances futures methodology beyond human-authored scenarios. It offers a transferable approach for comparing incremental and transformative pathways and reframes cities as collaborative environments in which multiple forms of life actively shape shared futures.
